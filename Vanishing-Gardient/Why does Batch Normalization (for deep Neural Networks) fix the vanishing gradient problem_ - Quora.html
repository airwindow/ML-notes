To clarify, the vanishing gradient problem is seen when the activation function for the neural net gets saturated. This is usually solved by using rectified linear units (ReLU) as the activations. Batch normalization is proposed so that more flexibility can be provided during the choice of the activation function. The resulting neural net after batch normalization is also more robust, in that they are less prone to divergences caused by the choice of initializations or from faster learning rates being used for the network.

Now to answer the original question about the vanishing gradients. Consider a sigmoidal activation function =(+)z=g(wu+b) for a neuron somewhere in the hidden layers of the neural network, u being the layers input, and w, b the weights matrix and bias vector respectively for the layer. The activation function ()g(x) is given by ()=11+()g(x)=11+exp(x). As the value of |||x|where =+x=wu+b increases, the term ()0g(x)0 . Hence the term vanishing gradients. Too high a value of x and we say that the activation function is saturated.

The graph above shows the gradient of the sigmoid moving towards zero on either direction, as the value of |||x| increases.

Notice that the value of x depends not only on a combination of weights and biases but also the layer input term u, obtained as a result of the distribution of the layers preceding the unit being analyzed. It is crucial to understand that the larger the means and variances of the layer input term, the higher the probability of the activation function to become saturated.

During training, the parameters of the network also naturally change. This causes a change in the distribution of the network activations (called the inverse covariate shift).

What batch normalization actually achieves is to reduce this inverse covariate shift. It achieves through a normalization step, in which the layer inputs are transformed into a form that ensures zero mean and unit variance, thereby eliminating the vanishing gradient problem. This series of transformation steps is what can be seen in Algorithm 1 in the paper.

Batch normalization prevents the gradients from getting too small or too large by normalizing data across each batch, as the name suggests. It also acts as a regularization method, similar to dropout.
