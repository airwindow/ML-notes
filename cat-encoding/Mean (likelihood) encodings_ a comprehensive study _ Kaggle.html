    Thanks for this interesting study!

    I found a potential bug with the way you do one hot encoding. Maybe you can confirm or not.

    I assume you use the second version of def onehotencode(traindata, testdata, columns) which does it all at once rather than column by column.

    The potential issue here is the way you use pd.get_dummies.

    You pass in prefix='onehot' . My observation is that when you don't pass a prefix, getdummies will prefix the encoded columns with the original column name. But if you pass in a single prefix, getdummies prefixes only with the specified prefix.

    So if we have a category such as "other" that appears in multiple columns, we get a duplicated column name "onehot_other". That is likely to cause issues down the road.

    The fix would be not to pass a prefix, or to pass a list/dictionary with prefix+col names.

    It seems like your first version of onehotencode might be doing the prefixing properly?

    If I am correct, you might get better results than currently with one hot encoding.

    Again thank you for your study! I am already using some of your helper functions on my project, which is how I stumbled upon the above.

    Viacheslav ProkopevKernel Author•Posted on Version 3•5 months ago•Options•Reply
    1

    α does not actually depend on a dataset size, as far as I understand. It makes encodings for smaller categories move closer to the prior (training dataset target mean), because we can not trust an encoding, that were estimated from like 2-3 samples.

    Bigger α can help, most likely in those cases when target distribution has bigger variance and when smaller categories are not very good predictors even if we get more samples for them.

    If you believe small categories in your data have predictive power, I suggest to try smaller α at first.

    I used a bigger α, up to 100, im my experience, α in a range from 1 to 10 works best.

    Viacheslav ProkopevKernel Author•Posted on Latest Version•4 months ago•Options•Reply
    0

    K-fold regularization makes CV a bit hard. No, you can not encode the whole data with K-fold regularization, it is only used for training data. Think about it, you do not supposed to know labels for a test set. If you do CV, on every iteration you will have a new train/test split. You encode train with K-fold regularization and test with simple train target mean (maybe smoothed using alpha parameter). So you going to have to do the whole mean encoding process on every iteration of CV. Take a train data, estimate the encodings for categories, use those estimates for test data and regularize the encoding for train data. You never regularize the test data (act like you do not know it's labels). So, just make sure you never use train labels on any step of the encoding estimation process.

    Thanks for this very interesting study that was for me an intro to mean encoding (and more generally target encoding).

    I have one comment though.

    For high cardinality features, one hot encoding produces a lot of columns. It slows down learning significantly and if we the model randomly samples a fraction of features for each tree or split, then the chances of one-hot encoded feature to be present in a sample are artificially increased, and chances of other (not one-hot encoded) variables to be considered on a split/tree are reduced. That makes a model treat one-hot encoded features as more useful than not one-hot encoded.

    This is indeed true for models out of the box which treat every variable equivalently when it comes to picking one to do the split. However, you could imagine having a model where, when picking a variable to do the split, you weight the probability to be picked by the inverse of the cardinality fo the original feature (or something along those lines). That would fix this issue but definitely not the slower training issue.
