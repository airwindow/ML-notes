Answered Sep 24, 2017 · Featured on HuffPost and 2 moreFeatured on HuffPost, Forbes, and Apple News · Upvoted by
Kaushalya Madhawa, PhD Computer Science & Artificial Intelligence, Tokyo Institute of Technology (2019) and
Nikhil Badugu, M.S Computer Science & Machine Learning, Northeastern University

As of 2017, many ML researchers are trying to crack the problem of “how do Deep Neural nets work, and why do they work so well in practice for problems we care about?”

Even if one does not care too much about the theoretical analysis and algebra, better intuitions of why DL works so well can help us make DL work even better for real-world applications.

The “Understanding Deep Learning Requires Rethinking Generalization” paper (Zhang et al. 2016) shows some intriguing properties of neural networks: specifically, they have enough capacity to memorize completely random inputs. In an SGD optimization setting, the training set error can be completely minimized with respect to an ImageNet-sized dataset where the inputs are all random noise (think white noise on your television set in place of images).

This runs counter to the classic narrative where “Deep Learning magically discovers lower level features, middle-level features, and higher-level features like the V1 system of the mammalian brain by learning to compress data”. From ~2012–2015 a lot of researchers used this “inductive bias” to explain how Deep Nets tend to have low test error, suggesting some form of generalization.

But if a deep net is also capable of memorizing random data, it suggests that the generalization capabilities are not entirely explained by the inductive biases of the model (e.g. the convolutional/pooling architecture, usage of regularization like Dropout, batchnorm), since these same inductive biases are also compatible with memorization.

Part of the reason this paper has received so much attention is the fact that it received a “perfect score” on the ICLR reviews and won ICLR2017 best paper award. That got a lot of people talking about the paper and asking themselves why the paper was so great, so there’s a bit of a feedback loop there. I do think it’s a great paper because it asked a question nobody was really asking, presented strong experimental evidence showing intriguing results.

However, I think it takes 1–2 years before the DL research community can really come to a consensus on whether a paper is important or not, especially because many insights are not analytically proven but rather determined empirically over many experiments, sort of like how it’s done in experimental neuroscience.

As Tapa Ghosh points out, some researchers argue that even though deep nets can memorize, that may not be exactly what they are doing in practice, since the time required to “memorize” a semantically meaningful dataset is shorter than the time required to “memorize” a random dataset, suggesting that deep nets can exploit semantic regularities in the training set when it is present.

I was also inspired by the “Sharp Minima” paper also presented at the ICLR2017 conference which showed that sharp minima found by batch gradient descent had high generalization error. Then a few months later Dinh et al. showed that local minima that generalize well can be made arbitrarily sharp [1703.04933] Sharp Minima Can Generalize For Deep Nets. While it does not completely invalidate the claims made by the Keskar et al. paper, it does suggest the picture is more complicated. Similarly, I think that while Zhang et al. 2016 has the potential to be an extremely important direction in understanding how Deep Nets work, it does not solve the generalization question of Deep Nets. Other researchers may soon publish work that challenge the ideas presented in the “Understanding Deep Learning Requires Rethinking Generalization” paper. Such is the nature of experimental science.

Succinctly, this paper is considered important because it shows that deep nets learn random datasets by memorizing them (zero generalization). This then begs the question of how it learns non-random datasets.

My own opinion on this generalization business:

It’s not altogether surprising that a high-capacity parametric model with a well-conditioned optimization objective (ReLUs, Batchnorm, high-dimensional spaces) will just soak up the input data like some kind of data sponge. I think of Deep Nets optimization objectives as an extremely “lazy” but powerful optimizer: it will discover semantically meaningful feature hierarchies if the right model biases are present and compatible with the input data, but if it isn’t convenient to optimize that solution, the network is perfectly happy to optimize in a way that just memorizes the data. Right now we just lack the means to control the degree of memorization vs. generalization, short of using blunt tools like weight norm regularization and dropout.

Because no one under 45 remembers how Generalization in Neural Networks actually works.

The paper points out an important property of neural networks that differs from supervised methods like SVMs in that Deep Nets always seemingly fit any data set, even if you randomize the labels. And you can not repair it through regularization. In an SVM, this does not happen. Say you have a good data set, and you can train an SVM with say, 90% training accuracy, Then, obviously, the SVM generalization accuracy, on some test set, is bounded by 90%. If you then randomize say 10% of the labels, and you retrain your SVM, you may over train and spuriously get a 90% training accuracy again. But generally we expect that we can avoid overtraining by tuning the regularization parameter, to get the best generalization error on the test set. And we expect the tuned training and generalization accuracies to be bounded by 90–10 = 80%.

In this paper, it appears that, if we train a neural network on a data set, and then randomize a large portion of the labels, that same neural network will fit the data very well — even with the random labels! And, presumably, there is no way to regularize the neural net — it always , eventually, over trains on the data set. Even though many of the labels are random.

And this seems to violate what we think we understand about how regularization works. And we need some new kind of theory.

To me, and here I am going out on a limb, it is fairly obvious to see what is going on in this paper if you use traditional Gardner theory (i.e. stat mech of spin glasses circa 1985) to understand their results.

Statistical Mechanics of Learning: A. Engel, C. Van den Broeck: 9780136493853: Amazon.com: Books

This ancient theory says that the neural networks are described by phase diagrams, and that classifiers can enter a spin-glass phase when there are many more network parameters than training parameters.

I imagine that every Deep Net has some kind of phase diagram with a spin glass phase where learning breaks down.

This was all worked out in the 90s and there are lots of old results on the phase diagrams of small networks. See, for example, this classic reference, Figure 3 , 7, and 8: (Figure 3a shown below)

http://clm.utexas.edu/compjclub/...

I propose that by randomizing a portion of the labels, you effectively reduce the number of training examples (p) without changing the number of parameters (N) (alpha = p/N in the phase diagrams above)

You don't want way more parameters than training examples.Or at least about as many . If you have too many parameters, things go bad. In the words of Jon von Neumann

With four parameters I can fit an elephant, and with five I can make him wiggle his trunk.

Lowering alpha moves the network into a spin glass phase, and the generalization error increases, while the training error remains near zero. I show this above.

Note—this looks nothing like simple generalization bounds from VC theory. There is a cutoff, and the system just collapses.

Now doesn’t this just like what they are describing in this paper ?

(quite frankly. this paper is so poorly written I can barely tell myself)

In fact, what they describe sounds exactly the glassy regime of a spin glass. The glassy phase is characterized a huge number of degenerate minima, separated by large energy barriers. In other words, the energy landscape becomes pathologically non-convex. Each minima corresponds to a solution with a random labelling. And there are as many minima as there are possible labelings…each one with zero training error ! Each one equally overtrained.

Additionally, they are obviously incorrect when they say there is no regularizer that works—that is, which gives a lower training accuracy. The regularizer they need is the familiar one—the stopping time. In the traditional sense, this looks like a temperature control. That is, as we move down the Energy Landscape, it takes longer and longer to converge because we have to ‘hop’ over barriers (either due to local minima and/or saddle points, which dominate in the glassy phase). And, of course, in the glassy phase, the convergence times slow down. This is why early stopping works as a regularizer in regimes of seemingly overtraining.

Answered Mar 22, 2017 · Upvoted by
Steven Sagaert, master Artificial Intelligence, KU Leuven (1997)

I’ll discuss the relationship between regularization as traditionally defined in mathematics and regularization as now commonly used in machine learning. Further, I’ll attempt to connect the current definition of regularization in machine learning with the observations of the paper Zhang et al. 2017 "Understanding Deep Learning Requires Rethinking Generalization." I won’t make any attempt to be exhaustive in the discussion of machine learning regularization, but will cite some examples without bias.

Historical regularization

Regularization had its origins in the computation of inverse problems in linear matrix algebra, such as might arise in systems of linear equations. Tikhonov regularization is one of the original types of regularization and addresses ill-posed linear problems, that is, problems where the inverse of a matrix is nearly singular. These are linear matrix problems of the form

𝑋𝛽=𝑌Xβ=Y

where one is estimating 𝛽β as b using matrices of observed X and Y. Each row of X are the observed input values of the variables forming the columns of X, and the corresponding Y row is the resulting output observation. Then, given an observation row matrix x, one can predict y as 𝑦̂ y^ by

𝑦̂ =𝑥𝛽y^=xβ

As is well-known, a solution for b is

𝑏=𝑋†𝑌b=X†Y,

where 𝑋†X†is the well-known Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse.

However, if X is nearly singular, there are severe stability issues with the resulting coefficients in b. This near-singularity causes the resulting coefficients to be very sensitive to noise. Tikhonov regularization is essentially expanding the linear model to include noise, then estimating that noise along with the linear coefficients. The simplest noise assumption is the noise variance is the same for all variables. This leads to 𝜎2𝐼σ2I type regularization, which reduces the sensitivity of the coefficients to noise by causing shrinkage of the small coefficients somewhat similarly to James-Stein shrinkage. Another way of thinking about this is that 𝜎2σ2 is the Bayesian prior of the noise, causing the results to shrink towards a value consistent with that prior.

Now instead of the previous solution, this gives

𝑏=(𝑋′𝑋+𝜎2𝐼)−1𝑋′𝑌b=(X′X+σ2I)−1X′Y

Here, I is the identity matrix. The value of 𝜎σ is typically determined by finding the value that minimizes the prediction error (essentially CV if the prediction is on a different data set than the fitting), or using more heuristic methods like L-curves (see for instance Hansen at Regularization Tools)

Later, non-linear systems of equations were accommodated, typically by simply linearizing the equations to be solved at a desired operating point, and this allowed systems of nonlinear partial differential equations to be solved.

Such regularization methods are well-grounded in multivariate statistics and numerical analysis, and the meaning of the model is explicit in the defining equations. A somewhat similar method of regularization, SVD truncation, gives similar results and for similar reasons - in both cases, the effect of singular vectors with singular values that are small is being reduced. In the case of Tikhonov regularization, the singular values are being shrunk by the noise covariance prior - something like a soft threshold. In the case of truncated SVD, singular values that are small compared to the noise covariance are being set to zero, not merely shrunk, so it conforms to a hard thresholding function.

Sparseness is a form of traditional regularization which sets insignificant coefficients to zero using a method similar to hard thresholding. It is used in such methods as LASSO. Sparseness as a regularization method can still be implicitly be connected to a prior through the penalization function.

All of these mechanisms reduce the effective number of degrees of freedom that are being fitted by the models.
